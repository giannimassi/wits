# Steelman

From the Principle of Charity (Davidson, 1970s) + Dennett's 4 steps (Intuition Pumps, 2013).
Build the strongest possible version of an argument before critiquing it. If you can't
articulate the opposing view better than its proponents, you don't understand it well enough
to critique it.

## When to use

You're about to reject an argument, dismiss an approach, or push back on feedback.
Before critiquing, ensure you're engaging with the strongest version, not a caricature.

## Protocol (Dennett's 4 steps)

1. **Re-express the position** so clearly and fairly that its proponent would say
   "Thanks, I wish I'd put it that way."

2. **List points of agreement** — especially any that aren't obvious or widely shared.

3. **Mention anything you learned** from the position, even if you ultimately disagree.

4. **Only then** state your rebuttal or disagreement.

This order matters. Steps 1-3 build the steel man; step 4 is your response to the
strongest version, not the weakest.

## When steelmanning reveals you're wrong

If during steps 1-3 you realize the opposing position is actually stronger than yours,
say so. The point of steelmanning isn't to make your eventual critique look more
credible — it's to ensure you're engaging with reality. Sometimes reality wins.

## Output format

```
THEIR POSITION (steelmanned): [strongest version]
AGREEMENTS: [what they're right about]
LEARNED: [what this perspective taught me]
MY RESPONSE: [critique of the strongest version, not the weakest]
```
