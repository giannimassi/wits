# Inversion

From Jacobi ("Invert, always invert") + Charlie Munger. Instead of asking "how do I succeed?"
ask "how would I guarantee failure?" Then avoid those things. Often reveals risks and
failure modes that forward reasoning misses.

## When to use

You're stuck on a problem, or your forward approach keeps producing the same unsatisfying
answers. Flipping the question unlocks new angles. Also useful for stress-testing plans
and identifying non-obvious risks.

## Protocol

1. **State your goal** in one sentence. What are you trying to achieve?

2. **Invert it.** Ask: "How would I guarantee the OPPOSITE outcome? If I wanted to
   make this fail as badly as possible, what would I do?"

3. **Generate 5+ ways to guarantee failure.** Be specific and creative:
   - What actions would ensure failure?
   - What would I need to ignore or neglect?
   - What assumptions would I need to make (that turn out false)?
   - What shortcuts would be most damaging?

4. **Check your current plan against the failure list.** Are you accidentally doing
   any of the things that guarantee failure? This is where inversion earns its keep —
   the overlap between "things that guarantee failure" and "things I'm currently doing"
   is often non-empty.

5. **Extract principles.** Turn the inverted failure modes into positive guidelines:
   "To guarantee failure, ignore testing" → "Testing is load-bearing in this plan."

## Output format

```
GOAL: [what you're trying to achieve]
INVERTED GOAL: [how to guarantee failure]
FAILURE RECIPES:
1. [specific action/inaction that guarantees failure]
2. ...
OVERLAP WITH CURRENT PLAN: [which failure recipes am I accidentally following?]
PRINCIPLES EXTRACTED: [what to protect or change]
```
